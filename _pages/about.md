Why do coercive threats fail even when sender states possess substantial material leverage? How do leaders, institutions, and political environments shape the signals states send—and how those signals are interpreted abroad? What happens to international security when emerging technologies become instruments of competition?

These questions guide my research in international relations, international security, and foreign policy analysis. I am a Ph.D. Candidate in Political Science at the [University of Wisconsin–Milwaukee](https://uwm.edu/political-science/), where I expect to complete my degree in May 2027. My dissertation, **Why Coercion Fails?**, examines how coercive signals are produced within sender states, transmitted through political and media environments, and interpreted by target elites and audiences. The project connects international security, foreign policy, and authoritarian politics through a mixed-method research design.

My broader work studies leader psychology and foreign policy, emerging technologies and international security, and the strategies middle powers use amid great-power rivalry. Methodologically, I combine statistical analysis, computational text analysis, survey research, and qualitative evidence. My articles have appeared in *International Political Science Review*, *International Relations*, and the *Journal of Security Sciences*, with additional chapters published by Routledge, Wiley, and the Turkish Academy of Sciences.

My research has received support from the [Institute for Humane Studies](https://www.theihs.org/), the ICPSR Summer Program in Quantitative Methods at the University of Michigan, the University of Wisconsin–Milwaukee, and the Middle East Foundation. I have taught comparative politics as instructor of record and assisted courses across international relations, comparative politics, global studies, and American politics.
